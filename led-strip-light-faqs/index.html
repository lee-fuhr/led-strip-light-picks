LED strip light questions answered

Updated June 2026Independently researchedNo paid placement.

Quick answer

LED strip lights vary widely in brightness, color options, and durability. Focus on lumens per foot, color temperature (2700K–6500K), IP rating for moisture, and voltage compatibility with your setup. Most standard strips run on 12V or 24V and can be cut at marked intervals.

What brightness do I need?

Brightness for LED strips is measured in lumens per foot (or per meter). A typical indoor strip might deliver 200 to 500 lumens per foot, which is roughly equivalent to a standard incandescent bulb every few feet. For accent lighting under cabinets or behind a TV, 150–300 lumens per foot is usually enough. For task lighting, like under-kitchen-cabinet work areas, aim for 400 lumens per foot or more. Don’t rely on wattage alone. Two strips with the same wattage can produce very different light output depending on the efficiency of the LEDs. Instead, check the lumen rating and read owner reviews that mention real-world brightness. Also note that dimmable strips give you flexibility to lower brightness when you want a softer glow.

How do I choose the right color?

LED strips come in three main categories: single-color (usually white in a specific temperature), tunable white (adjustable from warm to cool), and RGB or RGBW (full color plus white channel). For general living spaces, many people prefer a warm white around 2700K–3000K; for garages or task areas, a cooler 4000K–5000K works well. Tunable white strips let you change the mood throughout the day. If you want color effects, RGB strips are common, but RGBW (red, green, blue, plus a dedicated white LED) gives truer whites than standard RGB. Be aware that cheaper RGB strips often have noticeable color shifts and poor white balance. Read reviews that describe color accuracy and maximum brightness of each channel.

How do I install them properly?

Installation starts with a clean, dry surface. Most strips come with a 3M adhesive backing, but that adhesive can fail on textured walls or in high heat. For a permanent install, many experienced users add a thin bead of super glue or use aluminum channels with a diffuser and mounting clips. Measure your run length and plan for corners – some strips can bend slightly, but sharp 90-degree turns usually require a connector. Always power off the strip before cutting or connecting. You can cut most strips at the marked copper pads (usually every 1 to 4 inches depending on the density). After cutting, you will need a solderless connector or careful soldering to attach wires. Test the connection with the power supply before mounting the strip permanently.

What power supply do I need?

The most common voltages for LED strips are 12V and 24V. 24V strips suffer less voltage drop over long runs, so they are better for lengths over 16 feet or so. To choose the right power supply, add up the total wattage of all your strips (look at watts per foot) and then add about 20% overhead. For example, if your strip run uses 48 watts total, a 60-watt power supply would be a safe choice. Be careful with cheap “wall wart” adapters – they can buzz or overheat if pushed near their limit. Many LED enthusiasts prefer a metal-enclosed “LED driver” that can be hardwired or plugged in. Also check if the power supply is dimmable if you plan to use a dimmer switch. Always match the voltage exactly: a 12V strip on a 24V supply will burn out quickly.

Can I use LED strips outdoors?

Yes, but only if they have a suitable IP (Ingress Protection) rating. For dry indoor use, IP20 is fine. For a covered porch or bathroom (away from direct spray), look for IP65, which is protected against low-pressure water jets. For direct rain or garden use, IP67 or IP68 is ideal, though those strips are often encased in silicone and can be harder to cut or bend. Even with a high IP rating, avoid submerging connectors or the power supply unless they are rated for it. Outdoor strips also need UV-resistant coating to prevent yellowing over time. Check owner feedback for long-term outdoor performance; many budget “waterproof” strips start to discolor within a year in direct sun.

How do I connect multiple strips or extend a run?

You can connect multiple strips end-to-end, but voltage drop will limit the total length. For a 12V strip, runs longer than about 20 feet often show noticeable dimming at the far end. 24V strips can go 30 to 40 feet without serious voltage drop, depending on the strip’s current draw. To go longer, you need to inject power at multiple points (parallel connection) rather than daisy-chaining. Use L-connectors, T-connectors, or extension cables designed for your strip’s width (usually 10mm or 12mm) and pin spacing. Some strips use a 2-pin or 4-pin connector depending on whether they are single-color or RGB. Solderless connectors are convenient, but they can be finicky – many experienced users prefer soldering for reliability. Always double-check polarity before powering on.

Frequently asked questions

Can I cut LED strip lights to any length?

You can only cut at the marked cut lines (usually every 1 to 4 inches). Cutting anywhere else will break the circuit and ruin that segment. Measure your run carefully before cutting.

Do I need a controller for RGB strips?

Yes. RGB and RGBW strips require a controller to change colors and effects. Many come with a small remote or smartphone app. Make sure the controller matches the strip’s voltage and pin configuration (common RGB uses 4 pins: red, green, blue, common anode).

Why do my LED strips flicker?

Flicker usually comes from an incompatible or underpowered driver, loose connections, or a dimmer that is not designed for LEDs. Try a dedicated LED dimmer and ensure the power supply can handle the total load. Cheap PWM dimming at low brightness can also cause visible flicker.

How long do LED strip lights last?

Most quality strips are rated for 30,000 to 50,000 hours of use, which is many years of daily use. However, heat kills them faster – avoid enclosing strips in tight spaces without ventilation. Cheap strips may lose brightness or shift color long before their rated life.

Can I use LED strips with a motion sensor?

Yes, but you need a motion sensor that works with low-voltage DC power or a relay that switches the strip’s power supply. Some smart controllers include built-in motion sensors. Be mindful of the sensor’s detection range and placement.

In shortThink of LED strip lights as a custom lighting project: match the brightness and color to your space, choose the right power supply, and pay attention to installation conditions. A little planning upfront saves you from flickering lights or a burnt-out strip. Start with a small test run before committing to a whole room, and you will get the glow you want without the headache.
